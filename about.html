About the Author

I am Thomas Agulto and I currently attend the Northern Marianas College as a student, and work-study. My Taekwondo journey started in my Master's garden during the Covid-19 lockdown. After about 2 months of training in the garden, I was finally brought into the dojo to train and was rewarded with my first rank of white belt. Through the course of a year, I was able to reach the last colored belt, known in Korean as "Gup" or 2nd Gup which is the red colored belt. I forgot to mention that my brother was part of the journey. We did not outrank each other because we both wanted the same thing - achieve the black belt together.

Camacho Taekwondo

The Camacho Taekwondo is focused mainly on balance and coordination which in turn generates confidence. The Camacho Taekwondo has been in business for more than 10 years and is lead by none other than Congressman Vicente Camacho. Master Camacho has been practicing taekwondo for 45 years and has been teaching for over 25 years. He holds a 5th degree black belt and is certified under the World Taekwondo Federation.

Types of Training

The most common type of training done in the dojo would be peer instruction. This allows colored belts to either help or to teach the new students what to do in the absence of our master or senior ranking students or instructors. My brother and I have both started off with peer instruction before becoming assistant instructors.

Another kind of training is the balance training. We typically learn kicks on a flat surface. The best way to test our ability to balance is with the use of the balance beam. We are instructed to perform the kicks while walking on a beam similar to the ones that gymnasts use. This helps us to distribute our weight and execute the kicks properly.

Another training that was recently introduced to taekwondo as a whole is the use of the wooden staff (or stick). This is a common tool is used by most martial arts to defend oneself with. We use the staff to perform attacks and forms, which will be discussed in the next paragraph.

Forms, also known in Korean as "taegeuk" is a drill of techniques that can be used as a tool of self-defense. The forms involve multiple blocks and can help with balance and coordination. The forms are performed in a cross, turning left and right four consecutive times before transitioning to the next set of blocks. This is a pattern of defense that each student can use when in danger or feeling threatened by an aggressor.

Another training would be the self-defense drills. The drils involve a series of stand-up defense techniques that can stun then opponent and cause them to fall. These techniques does not require a whole bunch of stength, rather, momentum and wrist control to gain the advantage in any altercation with the element of surprise.

And lastly, one of the many trainings is the hand-to-hand combat. This type of training allows for all students to have some way of defending themselves if cornered by an aggressor. The types of hand-to-hand combat include effective take-downs and body shots that can hurt or stun the aggressor. One of my favorite hand-to-hand combat defense drills would be what we call "the bear hug". The bear hug is when an aggressor grabs the torso fom behind and is acted upon with the defender shrugging their shoulds, throwing an elbow to the rib, and grabbing the aggressor's arm and throwing them forward using momentum generated from the hips alone.
